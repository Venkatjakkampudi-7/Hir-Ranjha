Episode 01: Rabbi Shergill talks about Qissa

By Rajni Mujral / 16 September 2017 / 1:30:20

Follow the conversation

Rajni Mujral: Thank you so much for agreeing for this interview. But just to let you know that this is being recorded for academic purposes and we would like to have your consent for the archiving purposes and for research purposes. Thank you so much so.

The questions that I have today are related to the genre of qissa and its performance, and specifically qissa Heer Ranjha. Or any other Qissa that you would like to refer to. What I would like to ask you first is,

What is your notion of Punjabi literary tradition and how were you initiated into this tradition?

Rabbi Shergill:Well, first and foremost, I don’t think it’s a tradition. I think it’s an assortment of many traditions. It’s not simply one tradition. You could even argue that this is essentially what we are dealing with. It is a way of speaking, a way of communicating in Punjabi language itself and I was initiated into it the moment I was initiated into speaking Punjabi, so I think. Wait, what is your question? Centrally it is dealing with the anthropology of Punjabi language itself, I would think. I speak Punjabi and I’ve had the good fortune of learning from people who came from a culture which was still in much better health and vitality than what it is right now. I’ve been initiated into this it through my parents and I think there are many traditions. But If I specifically refer to qissa tradition, I like to think that it is an inherent form popular in many cultures, but more so my own. We just have this affinity for storytelling. You read about other cultures and you get to know this. I remember being curious about the indigenous cultures of Andaman’s and other indigenous cultures in and around India. They disseminate their way of being through stories. It’s unique to the human experience. Human beings like to tell stories. It is who we are and I think that is why we are the only species which somehow tends to have a culture, most of the primates don’t. Most primates are an anthropic being or a species being. But in addition to having a species being, I think we also have culture. That is why most human cultures vary from each other. Most monkeys of one species will not differ in their ways of being from other species. I mean the red macaque in Andhra would be the same as the red macaque in Delhi or North. But I think human beings are unique in the sense that in addition to having a sort of species being an anthropic being, they also have this need to tell stories. I think language is primarily unique to us and it affords us the unique luxury of sharing our experiences. Maybe other species do that telepathically. I have no way of knowing that, but I find it interesting that we are the only people who can also mould time simply by telling a story. The stories told by my parents have transported me back and forth. The question you asked me cannot be answered solely on the basis of my initiation into one particular culture. I think we will have to take into account the human story as well. We just like to tell stories. So, the question you’re asking is essentially: why do we tell stories? Telling stories is unique to us, the way it is unique for a snake to crawl or a lion to live in pride or to tiger to live solitary.

I’ve had the good fortune of having a smart father. I was born in the 20th century, but through my father, I experienced the old times as my father was rooted in the 18 th century. My dady (paternal grandmother) was rooted in even older times. So, I grew up with these old people. My father was 44 years old when I was born. I was born late to him. In a sense, he was my grandfather. When I came of age, he was already an oldish guy. So I got a sense of how life must have been in the 18th century, and it helped that he himself was a qissakar. He was a preacher and his speciality was the exegesis of the sikh tradition, even he was an exegit. He did this professionally. He was a qissakar, which is essentially just another word for qissagayaki. He spoke about the sacred religion. But through that, he’s expounding on his speciality here, and I could look at the auxiliary literature and traditions in and around Punjab and largely India. This is my tradition. I should say this because maybe through this, future researchers might get a sense of where I am, what my grid is. My father went to a school in Satto wali Gali (name of a street) near Amritsar and he had the good fortune of learning from Bhai Amir Singh Ji who was regarded as an enlightened master. As part of his course, he studied the exegesis of the two main granths: Adi Granth (aka Shri Guru Granth Sahib Ji) and Dasam Granth. In addition to that, he studied Quran, Tarek Anjeer and Vedas. My father was a polymath in the Oriental tradition. He could speak many languages including Farsi, Punjabi, Urdu and Sanskrit. Through him, I learned that we essentially have an oriental way of being. This is generally not the story that we are told: of being primitive, ahistorical people, part of the Hegelian project or the colonial project that encountered us, slaughtered us in a certain way. Through him and through the academic adventures of my mother, who, God bless her, was the principal of Mata Sundari College with an expertise in the field of comparative religion. She wrote many research papers on Adi Granth. Her doctoral thesis was on the textual arrangement of Adi Granth. Through her and through my father, through their conversations at home, I was initiated to the Panjabi tradition.

Let me respond to the next section of question i.e. my initiation to Heer Ranjha and several other qisse. One of the most cherished possessions of my life is a stone printed copy of qissa Heer Ranjha in Gurmukhi which must be, and seems like, a 1000 years old with unknown origin. It is written in a manner that the words were not separated from each other. This is how Gurbani and other literature was written earlier. These were contiguous, with words joined together, and not separated from each other with a space. Even the old Gurbani was written in this manner. You might even find the old copies of Adi Granth in which words are contiguous. It’s said that Bhai Mani Singh created the new version/system that we are used to right now. My father explained to me the first few chapters of Heer Ranjha. I didn’t realize it at that point, but that was fabulous and fantastic. He was a religious guy. I mean, he was formally a religious figure, but he was also a farmer, and farmers are essentially informal people. He was a very chilled out character and understood the spectrum of human nature and made allowances for it, despite being a formal religious person. He initiated me to Heer Ranjha and Heer Ranjha is a scandalous text. Back in his day, people were killed for singing aloud Heer Ranjha at the village well while taking a bath, and there were scraps that broke out. It was considered the way we might consider some pornographic material right now. It was an act of great academic liberty; I would think that for him. What I mean is that the kind of grid he came from and how he felt that he should also help me discover Heer Ranjha, that is revolutionary. I remember, and I should say this, it might sound a little scandalous or serious to your listeners here though. When the text begins, at the very beginning of Heer Ranjha, there is an explicit description of Heer’s physiology. I was a good student. I knew Punjabi language well. But when it came to meaning of certain body parts, I was just lost. Those words were from another era or another grid and I didn’t know what they meant. I remember stopping and asking my father about it. I remember my father taking some pregnant pauses and then explaining to me what those words meant. That I felt was one of the most cherished memories of my life. He didn’t think there was anything wrong as such descriptions are there in Gurbani as well. They also occur in Purans and Upanishads. My father who had gone through the entire gamut of literature, my father could distinguish that this was poetry, despite some explicit material in here, it was not of some garden variety. He knew that there was indeed something of value. My father, you know, he’d be lost in the city, but he could distinguish poetry from dribble and he was passing that on to me. I go back to it even now. It is the foundation, one could say, of Punjabi life. If anybody wants to understand Panjabi way of being, understand themselves and their own ontology, I think they would have to look at Heer Ranjha. The kind of encyclopaedic references to everything that we obtain therein are essential to understanding your own self.

I had the good fortune of going to Pakistan a few times. I remember the first time I went there in 2003 and I didn’t go to Lahore and we passed through that. We were in Karachi. Even in Karachi, the kind of people I met, the kind of facial features I observed, I could see that they were me and they weren’t me. In many ways, it was a story of the Punjabi soul- although those people weren’t Punjabis: some were Pathans, some were balochs. This opened up many windows for me. I remember coming back and telling my friends that anyone who wants to understand India will have to understand Pakistan as well. We’ll have to go there and see for ourselves, at least for North Indians. Guru Nanak (first sikh guru) never saw these boundaries. To be honest, I would say that, for me, there are these zones of resonances, and for me they exist more to the north, not Ladakh though. I’ve been to Ladakh and I love it, but the it’s not a zone of great resonance for me, spiritually. I understand there is Buddhism and there is all there is. But there’s something in the planes too, in the Cis Himalayas that somehow speaks to me. I live in Himachal, part of the year and it speaks to me. I love it. I just love it. More I move forward, more I understand a little bit of my own story. I mean, we have the R1A1 gene and somehow I have this great need to travel. I have a great need to see as far as the eye can see. It must be from Dagestan (land of the mountains), I don’t know. My father had this need. He was traveling in the 50s to Iran, Afghanistan. He just had this gumakkad (travelling) thing and I must have got it from him. I have the same thing. I have this need to get on my bike and just go somewhere. I think that it’s also the landscape, it’s also the language. For the longest time, I’ve argued that Punjabi has now become separated from the geography, but I’m not so convinced anymore. I don’t really think that it would change anything if you were to transport Punjabi to Canada, for instance. I mean, they have transported English to Americas, and they are what they are right now. So you could try doing the same to Punjabi, but I think you will lose some sort of organic connection to what it is. Maybe that’s bearing out in the Americas, those people don’t really care about the land.

They care about the language. The kind of civilization that they have developed is very different from the European civilization. The North American civilization is not really, if one has to think about nature, it’s not a default thought for it, it’s a deliberate act. I spend some time in Italy with my wife who is an Italian. Every time I go there, I realize that there’s something about them. These people have formed a balance with nature. They have come to some sort of understanding where they live in small houses, they beautify whatever little nook and cranny they inherit. They live in harmony. Italy doesn’t look like America: in the sense that I wake up in America and I’m horrified. It seems as if, overnight, an army of gardeners descended on that country and chopped all the grass and the hedges and just did something. It doesn’t seem like it’s breathing. You can see that the hand of man is everywhere. That’s not the case with Canada. Canada is the poor brother of America in some ways, so they let grass grow, a little bit. Canada is OK, but Canada just doesn’t know where it wants to be. I read somewhere that they want French culture, American politics, and English something and now it’s all mixed up in their culture. Those are the problems that you will have if you are going to be casual about your language. But I don’t even know if Punjabi is indeed a language. Is Gaddi (Indo-Aryan language of India, spoken by the Gaddi people primarily in Chamba district in Himachal Pradesh, India) a language we speak in Himachal and Chandigarh? It is considered a dialect of Punjabi, is it? Punjabi is just too vast. The differences are too many. Syntax varies so much. My mother was a Multani and I speak Majhi; is that even a thing? Are they even related? Multani is close to Haryanvi. It can go in many places. I don’t really know what it all really implies. I can only see some sort of commonalities and say that there is a zone of reference or resonance over there for me. That’s all I say. I can only speak about my little dialect. I and my friend Sanga, we keep scrapping all the time because I speak Majhi and he speaks Malwai. We take a piss out of each other all the time. So, Punjabiyat or we all like to think of it as a tradition, the term could even be a western term. The word we use is ravreet in Punjab and it’s a little more open ended. Things can flow in and out. The term tradition is more formal. That is in a sense a Punjabi way of being. You look at our civilization: you look at Harappa, you look at Mohenjo-daro, was it like Rome? What are the other civilizations that we can think of? Was it like the Egyptian civilization? Was it like Lothal in Gujarat?

It seemed like it hadn’t cordoned itself off from nature. You don’t find mass warfare. You don’t find those crazy patriarchal pathologies. In our culture, I don’t say Indian civilization, I would say Punjabi civilization. I don’t know what is Indian civilization; Hampi is very different from Harappa. But Harappa, I would argue, is Punjabi civilization. I would argue that Maharaja Ranjit Singh’s kingdom was a Punjabi nation. There is a whole bunch of contrived extrapolation which has happened in academia, and the more you understand it, the more you try to read about it, the more that notion doesn’t hold up. My mother is a Rajput. Through her, I look at the civilization of Rajasthan. These people felt great affinity for the Chauhans of Rajasthan, King Prithviraj Chauhan for instance. It’s magnificent. You look at Chittor, you look at Kumbhalgarh. Those people created that civilization and in such an arid landscape, and it still holds up. There’s still water, there’s still stuff that grows there and those things are magnificent. When you look at those, the thoroughfares are big. It’s not like the king made big thoroughfares for himself and smaller ones for the rest of the people. The moment you try to gain a sense of the political orientation of the people who built that, it seems vastly different from the political orientation which you can draw from the cities. There are no great slums in Kumbhalgarh or Chittor. It’s built along the contours of the land. The water is for everyone. It seems to be a celebration of nature. This is a far cry from what we have done now. I live in Delhi. I’ve grown up in Delhi. I have seen rolling hills from Delhi to Faridabad with my own eyes with little water streams and now it’s all flattened out. You make the inferences. We are disrupted people and the only way to really make amends is through language: the solution can only be found in local languages and not in formal Indian languages. Hindi is a strange language. It scares me. East India Company made it up. Even in some sense, Urdu is a made-up language. I don’t know how far we can go back. We have to start thinking anew. English has done greater damage to us. It has produced a class of kids who look at nature in a homogeneous category. For instance, any tree is a tree for them. They don’t even know the name of the trees. My father didn’t call a certain tree as rukh or drakhat (equivalent of the word tree). He would call it by its specific name. Once you have a specific name, it also has a specific purpose. It cannot be equated with another. It cannot be sacrificed so casually. There has to be some reason because a Peepal (Ficus religiosa) is not a Dharek (chinaberry tree) or Bohar (Banyan tree). Incidentally, my name is Bohar. When I was born, my dadi (paternal grandmother) named me Bohar. That’s the name I was given in my village. Bohar tree is considered sacred as it’s huge and everyone gathers around it. It’s difficult to replace a Bohar tree. There might be other trees: for instance, Kikkar (Acacia nilotica) has a specific purpose: it’s almost medicinal. Dhrek tree has a specific purpose and Mango tree has a specific purpose. Names give a life to their purposes: you would not be so cavalier with losing them. That is what English language has done for a variety of the natural phenomena that we see around us. It can all be categorized in sweeping categories, and it’s something that you find in Jharkhand these days. A plantation has become a substitute for a forest. You can kill the forest and create plantation there. It’s the same thing, because we only need these many trees in this much area. So if you have these many, they (government regulations) came up with some sort of category or parameter that if you have these many trees, it can be classified as forest land. This is what they’re doing in Himachal Pradesh. You can really see the generalization that creeps into your connection with nature by adopting an alien language. Although, English may not be that alien to us. Also, there are so many cognate words; I mean it’s not that we are speaking Mandarin which has a totally different logic. We have the same genetic structures and we have the same, I think, cognitive structures in our languages. Still, I feel that you are going to lose something. I find this with my kids, my kids are old I am fighting hard to keep a language alive. I twist and turn, and question myself: why am I trying to teach them Punjabi? Why am I trying to do this? The only conclusion I have is: it’s love. A person can only say that they love something if they have held on to their language; language is love. Maybe I am making some weird extrapolation here, but I feel that language is love. Your own language is love, it is the foundation and the cornerstone of your being. My kids sometimes wonder: why do I insist on speaking in Punjabi; why am I ramming it down their throats? All I say is: I am just trying to pass you something from millennia. We fought Alexander with this. You think we fought him with bow and arrow! No! Porus was a Punjabi speaking king and we fought him with this. Somehow if you speak Punjabi, you are going to become OK with confrontation: it is because we ask so many questions in the language. I don’t know if you know this: in Punjabi, we have a unique way of asking questions.

So every question is answered in a question:
Kī banā'i'ā,
ā gi'ā hō kē dēkha lā
(Conversation goes like this between two persons)
P1: what’s for food?
P2: Can’t you go and check yourself?
P1: Can’t you tell me?
P2: Am I your chef?

This is the kind of aggressive interaction that keeps happening in most authentic Punjabi households on a daily basis. This is my story. My father was confrontational. He kept asking questions. I learned to just keep asking him questions. So it was just questions. Sometimes, I’d say, I would count and we had given each other eight replies and they were all questions. If you speak Punjabi, in my view, you are going to be OK with confrontation. You’re going to have a little bit of swagger in your walk. I don’t know why. But you will. If you tie the turban (headgear) and the tying of a turban is a Political act, almost. It says: I am, I have rights, I have been. If you look at the act of a turban, and this is not a Punjabi thing, I mean it’s not a Sikh thing. It’s a Punjabi thing, first and foremost and the name for this is dastar (turban). It’s not a Punjabi word, it’s a Farsi word. With this, anyone can beautify himself. It doesn’t matter how ugly you are born. You look at Punjab and you look at the elaborate turbans that they tie: the beautiful fabric origami that they do every day in the morning. It is an act of asserting yourself, it’s an act of saying I am and you can’t push me aside. If you look at it, fundamentally, it’s such a such a simple, but such a beautiful act through which self-assertion has been just indoctrinated, almost subconsciously into essentially the downtrodden, the meeks. I’m digressing, I don’t know if I’ve answered your question!

Rajni Mujral: Yes, you are making a point and these are all interconnected points when it comes to language. For example, you are talking about Multani and Majhi language or even the combination of languages. The connecting point that I have in mind is about language in Waris Shah’s Heer: you find a combination of Multani and Majhi.

Rabbi Shergill:Waris Shah is essentially Majail: he lives in this region between these three Rivers Byas, Ravi and Jhelum. This is the region where I come from. When I read Waris Shah, for instance, instead of saying hai he says ee, uu, ki kita ee, ki kitaa uu (different helping verb conjugations). This is unique to us, the kind of conjunctions we make, for example: (come home) gharē āvī (in Punjabi) instead of saying ghara para ānā (in Hindi). It is a unique language and it’s just not Multani. My mother was Multani, the consonants do not aspirate (36:24/47). Somehow the timber is more Hindustani. The “T’’ sound is aspirated. Maybe, Madan Gopal (acclaimed performer of Heer) would know more about the correct technical specification here. There are these little interconnected windows that open up. Multani almost seems funny to me. It’s not a coincidence that the people from Lehnda, Lehnda is the Western Punjab, came and they settled in large numbers. One of the cities which received the most people was city named Jalandhar. If you go to Jalandhar, this has been my experience, you find that people are a little livelier in the city. In the village, they may be surly or even difficult people, that you find everywhere in Punjab, but in the city, you will find that people are little happier. If you go to Amritsar, people are not happy in Amritsar, people are just looking to scrap: tusāṁ dēkhē kivē mērē ala (how dare you look at me!). They are always twirling their moustaches. I personally find that people are happier in Jalandhar, the reason is: it has a great Lehnda character and people are a little more relaxed. They have this turn of phrase. They’re constantly making jokes. These guys like to joke and this is something also true of Pakistan. You would notice that when you see Pakistani programs. There is this great tradition of Jugat (Quibbling): Jugatā nā kara mērē nāla (don’t mess with me). We don’t have somehow a similar tradition or a similar trope here. There is this great to and fro happening all the time and this is something I recognize from my maternal side with my mamas (mother’s brothers). There was a constant banter, lively and warm banter. We also have banter in my village, but it quickly escalates to fisticuffs. There is some sort of civilizational evolution of language that existed, at least before 1947, which meant that you used language to mollify us. They used it to calm us down. They used banter and humor to distribute warmth to all stakeholders. Whereas this side, you find these people, maybe through the Marshall history, through resisting invasions or, just the Sikh character. They want to fight. If there is nobody else, they will fight each other. That is just who we are. I like to see that Waris Shah also does this through his language. He distributes the wide ontology, he sprinkles it in his Punjabi verse. Think of it, Ranjha is a Jat. Jat are strange people. They are not really royalty. They are a tribe. It’s anyone’s guess whether they are indeed a higher caste or lower caste. But you find this great assertion, even in Waris Shah, of Jat identity: Jat is a very curious case. Nobody considers them a high caste. It is they who consider themselves a high caste. It is done through self-assertion and self-propaganda. It’s one’s own backslapping. They have indeed succeeded in convincing everybody else of their high origins. I don’t think there are any more elevated than the other low castes that you find here. But you find it unique that they don’t care about it, and there’s something you see even right now. They use this word. If it was a bad word, they appropriated it. Maybe it’s like the niggar term. You appropriate it and take the sting out. We have done this. Pardon me for referring to this: Jat jhat sangatē: Jat gather quickly. It’s anybody’s guess where the word Jat comes from. It could come from the braided locks they wore when they were just transporting cattle across vast distances in Punjab. I find that very interesting. I like to think of my ancestors as people who were great travellers. They just wanted to travel the landscape. I was having a conversation with somebody else and you would say the Virk- one of the sub castes of the Jat- are great at understanding the landscape. They can find routes just by looking at the footmarks of the cattle. I find those stories magnificent. I think you find a crazy amount of those things in Waris Shah. Waris Shah himself is a Syed. He is Qureshi high caste Muslim and he is writing about these Jat characters. Look at the syncretism that existed in the society. Ranjha, a Muslim becomes a Yogi in an instant, and nobody seems to have a problem with it. He goes to him (a guru) and says, teach me. Guru says, you Jats are fools and you will never learn. You must do this. You must do that and tries to pass on to him is his pedagogical epistemology. Ranjha says, please stop here, I have come to you to take Yog, I am not here to listen to any bullshit. You should listen this: it’s not like the overly differential submission of oneself to a guru which you find in most indoctrinations. Ranjha says, I am here to learn a few things, don’t cross the line. It is a very strange thing. We keep thinking about it. Look how he invokes him. There is this Kuldeep Manak (20 th century Punjabi Singer) tune. I don’t think it’s Waris Shah’s work, but it’s a famous song in Punjab:

Tērē tīlē uṭhōṁ sūrata dīhadī ā hīra dī, āha dēkha gōrakhā uradī ā phulakārī.

Ranjha’s way of addressing the guru is not the most respectful invocation of one’s teacher. First, he calls him in lower terms in terms of respectability. Tērē tīlē uṭhōṁ sūrata dīhadī ā hīra dī: I can see the beyond from over there. Come gorkha, look there you can see the Phulkari. He must have been able to see or something the woman’s embroidered scarf. When You look at the sikh history thereafter, there are so many instances: you find that the khatris (name of a caste in Punjab) are very differential to the guru and you find the Jats just holding on to their ari (stubbornness). Ranjha Says, show me a miracle. Then I’ll come to your fold. Guru Nanak is able to find another Khatri because somehow khatris must have been schooled in. They must have a little bit of this insider understanding familiarity or what was required of them to become one with the guru. That is why Baba Buddha (prime figure in early Sikhism and Guru Nanak’s contemporary) failed the test. Guru Nanak was trying to find a suitable successor; he couldn’t see Baba Buddha as his successor. He couldn’t find it amongst the Jats. He hadn’t really put any caste barrier. He gave Baba Buddha, a Jat, a chance but Baba failed the test. The test was, the suspension of everything that you look; it was a complete suspension of one’s own cognitive perception. Guru Nanak asked Baba including his other disciples to go chew on a floating corpse. This was the last and final test and nobody was able to do this. Even Baba Buddha said, I can’t do this. This is when Bhai Lehna intercepted: he must have known something, something must have existed within him and he said, where should I start from? The head or the foot? That was the answer that got him Gurugaddi (seat of the guru, bestowing the seat of guruship upon new guru). Baba Buddha refused to eat the corpse, and said, I’m not going any further than this. There is this crazy story. Guru Nanak, on a stormy day, takes his disciples into low waters of a river. He wades through water and drags the corpse. He says, you guys will not leave me alone. I don’t want any one of you. OK? You will not leave me alone? If you want to stay by me, go eat the corpse. It is said that everyone except Bhai Lehna steps back. Baba Buddha, who is otherwise a great figure, serves six gurus, is perhaps, after gurus, the most important personality in Sikhism. Even he fails this test. I don’t know why I am landing up here: there is certain part of Punjabiyat as manifested in the Jat Sikhs, it is the inability to give themselves up. We hold on to this. It serves us well in some regards, or maybe it doesn’t. There is an analogy with the Krishnamurthy (20 th century Indian philosopher) story where he is raised to be Maitreya and he is going to be the Buddha Incarnated. At that moment, he is unable to give himself up. In that sense, this is something that I find in Punjabiyat in a crazy amount. We are unable to give ourselves up. It is not one caste, it is everyone. There is just so much pride, and not ego, but just pride. Ego is a personal thing. Pride is anchored in something larger than myself. Ego is just about I, me, myself. You find this everywhere that we are unable to give up. For instance, I cannot give up my own story. Somehow it has a caste apparition that I can’t give up my name Gill. Not because it’s convenient for me, it is convenient, for sure. These guys are farmers. There are Jats. They are everywhere. They’re politically, culturally preeminent. But at the same time, I cannot forget where we come from. In this world, I have a millennial’s old story. We are a tribe. We existed in very poor conditions. I have tales of extreme poverty related to me by my father. But I cannot also discount that poverty is not the worst thing that could have happened to you in 1940s or 50s in an Indian village. There is still a privilege and you can’t deny that there is privilege. It is a recent thing. I would argue that it’s a recent thing. We were referred to as chandals in the 12th century. I think Maurya (reference to Chandragupta Maurya) might himself be Jat. Look at us, we are good with animals. Who works with animals! It is not the high caste that work with animals. Look at Italy right now, what are we! The entire milk production industry is being handled by Jat Sikhs. These traits don’t look like the traits of any great high caste. These traits look like the traits of sons of soil who, through the benediction of the Gurus and through some cultural traits or crazy amount of self-pride, have been able to get somewhere. But I cannot discount that they have blocked others’ progress. They are in the way of the dalits (subjugated caste classified as a Scheduled Caste in India). But you find that dalits are also, in some ways now, mimicking the successful behavior of the Jats. They are appropriating the slurs. They don’t care about being called chamars, they say, munda chamar da (son of cordwainer). They are not scared of the words that were used to denigrate them. You’re only going to be able to produce culture, to produce life, if you have love. I think the single precondition for that is speaking your language. Your language, maybe it’s not a mother tongue, maybe it’s something else, but you are speaking it. We live in Delhi; we are five siblings. My eldest sister, poet of great eminence, writes in Hindi. And the Punjabi in me wants to know, why do you write in Hindi? But the Delhite in me says, why do I write in Punjabi? I come from the land of Ghalib and Mir (Poets). Why am I writing in Punjabi? What’s my justification for writing in Punjabi? Those are questions I think about, and I can only say this is love. I love my father. My truth is that the only love I’ve truly known is my father. Every time I’m lost, I go back to him. Through him, I go back to Punjab. I find myself in Punjab. Even if I find myself physically in Delhi, I find myself mentally in Punjab. That is my trapdoor of love. Because I grew up in Delhi where Hindi is second language for everyone, even for those people for whom it’s supposed to be their first language. So in Delhi, Hindi is a second language. We speak it badly. But when you go to maybe Jamia, when you go to Old Delhi, when you go to other of these old places with some Mughal antecedents, then you find maybe somebody speaking with some authentic flavour. So I got my love from Punjabi and you need love to create art. That’s my reason.

Rajni Mujral: I’m just thinking of what you said about Heer Ranjha and your love for poetry. I had an interview with Gurmeet Bawa ma’am, who has sung Mirza in different ways. So there was one incident where she recalls an event. (R.S. Gurmeet Bawa? From Amritsar? R.M. Yes). A week or so before she passed away, we happened to have a conversation over a call. That was supposed to be recorded, but it didn’t materialize. When I met her in Amritsar, she spoke about performing Mirza for a family. The family wanted her to perform qissa Mirza for seven days, the same qissa for seven days. She told me that that was one moment when she realized that if she has to sing the same qissa for seven days, she has to make some improvisation, which means the qissa cannot be the same on each day. There has to be some tweaks, there has to be some changes, there has to be some innovation that she has to do. I have listened to the composition Heer, that is available online, and that you perform and some other singers have performed. So in your case as well, is there any feedback that you get from the audience when you perform live? Do you think that has changed your performance in different ways?

Rabbi Shergill:I am a mutation. So I am a mutation in the sense that I speak about roots culture from Punjab. I come from Delhi. I am a public school-bred, university-educated, English-speaking guy. So, that alienates me from a vast majority of Punjab. They see some resonance with me, but they see also a disconnection with me. When I do my thing, they’re all sitting like they’re receiving high culture. So, they keep quiet in Punjab. In Delhi, we are booers. We just want to sing this song, sing that one, sing a hit, sing that. That’s generally been my experience that most people find me a little inaccessible. I don’t know how to change that. I am who I am. And I think it is important that I exist as myself. Because I think in some ways, I represent the times. I’m trying to not be. For me, being very rootsy would be an artifice. I grew up with the blues. I grew up with Jimi Hendrix. I grew up this guy, and I am so grateful for it. Because Punjabi music for the longest time was just driven to me. I used to call it a dead poet society. If you took away the dead poets, I say it even now, you take away the dead poets and what do you have? Do they have any new classics? You asked me this question. Do we have any new classics? Do we have any new qisse? Where are the new qisse? Do you think we have no story? Do you think there is no pathos? An entire country emigrating to a foreign land. Your water is gone, your topsoil is gone, you’re killing daughters in a frenzy. And there is no story. And still you want to see 48, 27, 48, weight kudi da (reference to a song that talks about a women’s twenty-eight inches’ waistline and forty-seven kg weight). Still you want To go there! It’s like we’re dying and we’re celebrating death. Where is the feistiness? There is nobody fighting. Pop music is not fighting. And if it’s fighting, it’s fighting a very niche war. Okay, Kanwar Grewal is doing all this. Great. Power to him. But it’s not Punjabi culture. It’s farmer culture. It’s not the ecology of Punjab. Ecology of Punjab has no takers. It has no representation in art. Art in Punjab is just not interesting. It’s about privilege. It’s about showing our privilege. Punjabiyat has no representation in its art. There are no Dalit voices. There are no Bhapa (Pejorative term for Sikhs who migrated from Pakistan) voices. There are no Punjabi Hindu voices. There are no Tarkhana (traditional carpenter caste) voices. There is nothing. There is just a boorish ad infinitum assertion of the Jat (dominant community) identity. It’s just boring. There is no nuance. There is no detail. It’s just a constant drone. It’s just a hammer. And I just have no patience for it. I don’t care how much money they make. I don’t care how much privilege they glean for themselves. It is meaningless. We are just having a marriage party in a house that’s in flames. How long does it take for you to realize you’re all living? All the singles who want to immigrate to Canada. All of them have a Canadian passport. They all want one leg there. Everyone who has any means has sent his kids abroad. Every single person! Who is fighting for Punjab. What does it even mean to say you fight for Punjab? Punjab is not its present Euro-modernistic grid. Without saving Punjabi rivers, without saving Punjabi forests, without saving Punjabi flora, fauna, there is no Punjab. I am sorry, but we have a very limited notion. If you’re a Jat Sikh who is single, you’re basically just talking about perpetuating your own culture. You’re not talking about Punjab. Punjab is huge. Look where it’s led us, this blinkered view of Punjabiyat. Look at us. We were a country in 1849 and here we are. Even Himachal doesn’t think they are Punjab. Himachal! You go to Kasauli, they all speak Punjabi. The songs that they sing are in Punjabi. But if you ask, is this Punjab? No, this is pure Hindustan. You look at what we have done to ourselves. It’s the same, I think, and the pop music that we have right now is only an intensification of the same orientation.

The poem that I used to relish the most when I was really young was Āsā tā jōbana rutē maranā (I will die young) and I still can’t get enough of that. Is it possible for you to talk about the qissa that you sang just now for me, Nooran?

Rabbi Shergill:It’s a story of Nooran. She is a little wild character, deviant character even. She can see that Shiv is disapproving of her. But he is also balanced. She is presented as promiscuous. The end of the story is presented as a sort of justification for what happens to promiscuous people. It’s a bit of that. It has some bit of Punjabi mores in it, good or bad. It starts with Rōja

But we disapprove when she becomes a deviant. That is a theme that you find everywhere. In all our epics, even Ramayana, they don’t like that Sita could paint Ram from memory. We tend to scoff and frown at a woman expressing herself. It’s not the same for the woman. But physiologically, she’s way stronger. In some ways, if it came to a life of promiscuity, then she’s built for it and the man is not. I mean, physiologically, he’s the weaker sex, in that sense. So there is, in this qissa, you find a theme of the limit of masculinity. A man can only do this and no further. What lies beyond that, he’s afraid of that. And what he’s afraid of, he scoffs at and he disapproves of. So, Noora is a sexual deviant.

Shiv Batalvi finds a plot point where she is given her just desserts at the hand of a young lover who does to her what she’s been doing to others. She turns into a rag on the floor after that. After he is done with Nooran, the poet says:

This staza is about after he is done with handing out her or meeting out her punishment. I find the end very interesting with regard to Punjabi culture. It’s a strange thing in Punjabi. We don’t have a happy folk song. We only have sad folk songs. All the great stories are sad. Challa is sad, Heer is sad, Mirza is sad, everything is sad. And then it talks about how the Punjabi way of looking at world, at cosmos, resolves the limits of perception, of human perception: Akhadē nē sāla kai hudē calē, aja tīka ōha dīvā bālē So now, Nooran becomes, from a real person, a ghost. We don’t know if she’s a ghost or whether she’s a human being, pretty much like that character in a Premchand story. Akhadē nē sāla kai hudē calē, aja tīka ōha dīvā bālē

Is that a real diva (lamp) or is it a diva that the poet is lighting through the verse in your hearts and mind? The first three-fourths of the poem deals with, the poet expresses his own limited Punjabiyat, good or bad, in very seductive imagery.

At the end, he does more justice to his genius as a poet when he starts talking about the limits of perception and how she’s both something present here and something present over there.

I find that to be an essence. I find that most Punjabi qisse are simply a preparation for the afterlife. You look at Hashim, you look at Pilu, you look at all this other literature also. It’s all a preparation. We are very interior people. There is an interiority in Punjabi culture which is grossly at odds with our extrovert persona. We are supposed to be very extrovert; we are supposed to be very talkative. When you try to engage with the language, there is this crazy interiority in there.
